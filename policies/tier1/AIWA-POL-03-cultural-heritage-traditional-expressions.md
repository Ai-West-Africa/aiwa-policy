# Cultural Heritage & Traditional Expressions Policy

**Document ID:** AIWA-POL-03
**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Active
**Tier:** 1 — Foundational Governance
**Scope:** AIWA's obligations, standards, and restrictions regarding cultural heritage, traditional cultural expressions, and community-origin materials.
**Restrictions:** This document does not contain process or workflow. Those are contained in AIWA-DOC-03.
**Cross-Reference:** AIWA-DOC-01 (Classification), AIWA-DOC-02 (Legal Alignment), AIWA-POL-04 (AI Governance), AIWA-POL-16 (Sensitive Cultural Materials), AIWA-STD-01 (Terminology)

---

## 1. Purpose

1.1. This document defines AIWA's obligations with respect to cultural heritage and traditional cultural expressions (TCEs).

1.2. This document establishes the distinction between creator-owned works and community-held traditional cultural expressions.

1.3. This document establishes the restrictions applicable to AI use, licensing, and exploitation of heritage and community-linked materials.

1.4. This document is the political and governance protection instrument for AIWA's cultural administration responsibilities.

---

## 2. Non-Ownership of Cultural Heritage

2.1. AIWA does not claim ownership over:

- languages;
- oral traditions;
- communal folklore;
- traditional knowledge;
- sacred practices;
- ceremonial forms; or
- cultural identity.

2.2. AIWA administers rights on behalf of rights holders and does not acquire ownership of copyrights, communal heritage, languages, or cultural identity except where expressly agreed in writing.

2.3. No AIWA agreement, decision, or licensing action may be construed as a claim of ownership over a TCE or community-held cultural expression.

2.4. AIWA acts as a designated steward for works in its system. Stewardship is not ownership.

---

## 3. Recognition of Cultural Heritage

3.1. AIWA recognizes that cultural heritage includes:

- intangible cultural heritage as defined under the UNESCO 2003 Convention;
- expressions of folklore as protected under Gambian law;
- traditional knowledge and associated practices of Gambian and West African communities;
- sacred and ceremonial forms held by specific communities; and
- oral genealogies, oral histories, and community narratives without individual authorship.

3.2. These forms of cultural heritage are held in trust for communities and peoples, not for individual administrators or commercial entities.

3.3. AIWA acknowledges the sovereign authority of Gambian institutions, including the National Centre for Arts and Culture (NCAC) and the Ministry of Tourism and Culture, over Gambian cultural heritage determinations.

3.4. Where a determination of cultural heritage status is made by a Gambian authority, that determination is final within its jurisdiction and binding on AIWA.

3.5. AIWA cultural classifications are operational and contractual tools used to manage intake, access, licensing, and safeguarding within the AIWA system. They are not sovereign determinations of national cultural status.

3.6. Where folklore status, sacred status, or national heritage designation is materially disputed, AIWA must maintain restriction or hold status and refer or defer the question to NCAC or another applicable Gambian authority.

3.7. AIWA supports, and does not replace, Gambian cultural institutions. Its role is to operationalize protections for materials that pass through the AIWA system.

---

## 4. Distinction Between Creator-Owned Works and Traditional Cultural Expressions

4.1. Creator-owned works and traditional cultural expressions are categorically distinct.

4.2. A creator-owned work is:

- authored by an identifiable individual or group;
- of modern origin regardless of cultural inspiration; and
- subject to conventional copyright protection.

4.3. A traditional cultural expression (TCE) is:

- of collective or community origin;
- without an identifiable individual author; and
- held in common by the relevant community.

4.4. Cultural inspiration does not convert a creator-owned work into a TCE.

4.5. A modern retelling, adaptation, or interpretation of a traditional story or cultural theme is a creator-owned work, not a TCE, provided it has identifiable authorship.

4.6. Where the classification is uncertain, the matter is subject to the classification and review process under AIWA-DOC-01 and AIWA-DOC-03.

4.7. No work is reclassified from creator-owned to TCE, or from TCE to creator-owned, without a formal written determination by the Classification Officer and, where required, the Cultural Review Unit.

## 5. Sensitive Cultural Materials Review

5.1. The following material types require Cultural Review Unit review before any licensing or exploitation decision:

- sacred recordings or representations;
- ceremonial performances;
- oral histories of community significance;
- materials from community archives submitted under restricted conditions;
- works identified by the submitting community as requiring restricted handling; and
- materials with unclear or disputed TCE status.

5.2. No sensitive cultural material may be licensed, exploited, or distributed before Cultural Review Unit clearance.

5.3. Cultural Review Unit clearance is a mandatory step, not an optional one.

5.4. The Cultural Review Unit must consult with applicable community representatives where practicable before issuing clearance for sensitive cultural materials.

5.5. Cultural Review Unit decisions on sensitive cultural materials may be escalated to the Governance Board by any party to the review.

---

## 6. AI Restrictions on Heritage Materials

6.1. No TCE or community-held cultural expression may be used for AI model training without:

- express written consent of the applicable community or designated authority;
- formal cultural review and clearance; and
- documented provenance confirming the legitimacy of the consent.

6.2. No AI system may be used to:

- synthetically reconstruct or recreate a TCE without authorization;
- impersonate or simulate a cultural identity, sacred voice, or ceremonial practice;
- create outputs designed to substitute for or replicate protected cultural expressions; or
- commercially exploit AI-generated imitations of protected cultural materials.

6.3. These restrictions apply regardless of whether the AI output is marketed as derivative, inspired, educational, or fictional.

6.4. Restrictions on AI use of heritage materials may not be overridden by a commercial licensing agreement or sublicensing arrangement.

6.5. AI restrictions on heritage materials are enforceable against sublicensees. AIWA must include these restrictions in all sublicensing agreements involving heritage-adjacent works.

## 7. Provenance and Attribution Standards

7.1. Every work with a cultural or heritage classification in the AIWA system must carry documented provenance records.

7.2. Provenance records must include:

- the origin community or cultural context;
- the submission authority and their documented relationship to the material;
- any known chain of custody for archival or historical materials;
- applicable cultural designations (e.g., sacred, ceremonial, community-restricted); and
- any consent documentation from community authorities.

7.3. Attribution standards for culturally affiliated works must acknowledge:

- the community of origin where applicable;
- the cultural tradition from which the work derives; and
- individual authorship where identifiable.

7.4. Attribution must not misrepresent community ownership as individual ownership or vice versa.

---

## 8. Community Benefit Principles

8.1. Where AIWA administers works of significant cultural value to a community, AIWA must:

- ensure that revenue from licensed use flows to the relevant rights holders or community fund;
- not derive disproportionate administrative benefit relative to community benefit;
- include community representatives in decisions about the use of community-linked works where practicable; and
- provide transparent accounting of revenues generated from community-linked works.

8.2. AIWA must not use community-linked cultural materials to generate revenue for AIWA's benefit alone.

8.3. Community benefit principles apply to royalty dormancy procedures under AIWA-POL-02. Where dormant royalties from community-linked works are transferred to a community benefit fund, AIWA must consult with applicable community representatives.

---

## 9. Educational Preservation Principles

9.1. AIWA supports the preservation and educational use of cultural heritage materials in accordance with Gambian law and applicable UNESCO frameworks.

9.2. Educational preservation use includes:

- archival duplication for non-commercial preservation;
- academic documentation and research;
- language preservation and documentation; and
- publication in educational materials with appropriate attribution.

9.3. Educational preservation use does not include:

- commercial exploitation;
- AI model training without separate authorization; or
- redistribution as a commercial product.

9.4. AIWA must apply the appropriate classification level before approving any educational preservation use.

---

## 10. Language Preservation Commitments

10.1. AIWA acknowledges that works in Gambian and West African languages represent irreplaceable cultural assets.

10.2. AIWA commits to:

- handling works in African languages with the same rigor and care as works in international languages;
- preserving language context, cultural meaning, and attribution in all processing;
- not degrading linguistic data through improper classification or processing; and
- supporting language preservation as a component of AIWA's educational resource mandate.

10.3. Translation, transcription, or linguistic analysis of works in African languages must comply with AIWA-POL-14.

10.4. No language documentation submitted to AIWA may be repurposed for commercial language modeling without explicit written consent from the rights holder and, where applicable, the relevant community authority.

---

## 11. Restricted Uses

11.1. The following uses are prohibited for all cultural heritage and TCE materials in the AIWA system without exception:

- political propaganda;
- hate speech;
- deepfake creation;
- pornographic adaptation or sexualization;
- cultural impersonation for commercial gain;
- deceptive synthetic recreation; and
- any use designed to exploit, misrepresent, or dishonor the source community.

11.2. These restrictions apply regardless of the requester's identity, stated purpose, or commercial relationship with AIWA.

11.3. A licensing agreement does not override these restrictions.

11.4. Violation of these restrictions constitutes grounds for immediate termination of the relevant licensing arrangement and referral to applicable authorities.

---

## 12. Governing Framework

12.1. This policy is governed by:

- Gambian law, including the laws of The Gambia protecting expressions of folklore;
- the UNESCO 2003 Convention for the Safeguarding of Intangible Cultural Heritage as interpretive support;
- WIPO frameworks on traditional cultural expressions as interpretive support;
- indigenous data sovereignty frameworks as interpretive support; and
- the African Union's cultural preservation principles as interpretive support.

12.2. Where these frameworks conflict, Gambian law governs.

12.3. AIWA is a steward and classifier. AIWA is not the sovereign authority over protected heritage questions. Gambian institutions hold sovereign authority.

---

## 13. Community Representation Standards

13.1. Where this policy refers to "community representatives," the following standards apply to determine who qualifies, how authority is verified, and how competing claims are resolved.

13.2. A qualifying community representative is:

- an individual or body formally designated by the relevant community through its established governance process;
- an institution recognized under Gambian law or applicable international frameworks as holding authority over the community's cultural heritage; or
- a person holding documented delegated authority from either of the above.

13.3. AIWA must not unilaterally appoint a community representative. Community representative status must be established through one or more of the following:

- formal documentation from a recognized community governance body;
- recognition by the National Centre for Arts and Culture (NCAC) or a designated Gambian authority;
- a legal instrument confirming representative authority; or
- a formal declaration by the community in a form acceptable to AIWA and reviewed by the Cultural Review Unit.

13.4. Authority verification requires:

- review of the documentation establishing representative authority;
- confirmation that the authority is current and has not been revoked;
- record of any conditions or limitations on the representative's authority; and
- documentation retained in the relevant cultural material's provenance record.

13.5. Where two or more parties claim community representative authority over the same cultural material:

- both claims must be immediately flagged as disputed in the AIWA system;
- no licensing, processing, or distribution decisions involving the disputed material may proceed until the dispute is resolved;
- the matter must be escalated to the Cultural Review Unit and, if unresolved, to the Governance Board;
- AIWA must not adjudicate the dispute but may facilitate its referral to an appropriate Gambian authority or recognized cultural governance body; and
- the material must remain under restricted access pending resolution.

13.6. AIWA must maintain a Community Representative Register recording all verified community representatives, the scope of their authority, and the materials to which their authority applies.

13.7. Community representative authority does not create rights in the material for the representative. Authority to participate in governance decisions is not equivalent to ownership.

13.8. A verified community representative may file a documented objection to a proposed licensing, AI-use, access, or distribution decision involving community-linked material. AIWA must record the objection, place the matter on hold or keep it restricted pending review, and escalate to NCAC or another applicable Gambian authority where the objection raises a disputed question of public cultural authority.
