## 1. Purpose

1.1. This document defines what constitutes a "work" within the AI West Africa (AIWA) system.

1.2. This document defines what constitutes "folklore" under Gambian law and AIWA policy, and establishes the legal and operational distinction between folklore and modern creation.

1.3. This document establishes classification categories, classification levels, and the boundaries that control how works are handled, monetized, restricted, and whether works may enter AI systems.

1.4. This document is the authoritative classification reference for all AIWA operations. All classification decisions must conform to the definitions and rules in this document.

1.5. This document does not contain process, workflow, or operational procedures. Those are contained in AIWA-DOC-03.

---

## 2. Definition of a Work

2.1. A "work" is any intellectual creation that is:

- fixed in a tangible form (written, recorded, captured, or otherwise made persistent);
- identifiable by title, content, or other descriptor; and
- submitted to AIWA through an authorized intake channel.

2.2. A work does not require publication or public release to qualify as a work under this definition.

2.3. A work may be submitted by an individual, a group, an institution, or an authorized representative.

2.4. The act of submission does not constitute a claim of ownership. Ownership is determined by the rights and classification review process defined in AIWA-DOC-03.

2.5. A work that cannot be fixed, identified, or accessed through the AIWA system does not qualify as a submitted work and does not receive a classification status.

## 3. Work Types

3.1. All works submitted to AIWA must be assigned one of the following work type designations. These designations align with Gambian copyright law and international intellectual property frameworks.

3.2. **Literary Work** — Any written or text-based creation, including books, ebooks, manuscripts, scripts, and written educational materials.

3.3. **Musical Work** — Any composition consisting of music, lyrics, or both. A musical work is distinct from a sound recording of that work.

3.4. **Sound Recording** — Any fixation of sounds, whether or not of a performance of a musical work. Includes audio recordings of spoken content, music, radio programmes, and language resources.

3.5. **Audiovisual Work** — Any work consisting of a series of related images which, when shown in succession, impart an impression of motion, with or without accompanying sounds. Includes films, video works, television programmes, and documentary recordings.

3.6. **Broadcast** — Any transmission by wireless means, including radio and television broadcast. A broadcast may combine elements of a sound recording and an audiovisual work.

3.7. **Educational Material** — Any work created primarily for use in formal or informal education, including textbooks, curricula, worksheets, and instructional recordings. Educational materials may also qualify as literary, audiovisual, or other work types depending on their format.

3.8. **Reference Work** — Any work structured for look-up purposes, including dictionaries, encyclopedias, directories, and glossaries. A reference work is owned only in its structure, selection, and arrangement. The language it documents is not owned.

3.9. **Dataset or Compilation** — Any structured collection of data, facts, records, or other materials which, by reason of the selection or arrangement of its contents, constitutes an intellectual creation. A dataset is owned only in its structure, selection, and arrangement. The underlying data points are not owned solely by reason of their inclusion.

3.10. **Language Resource** — Any structured collection of words, phrases, grammatical structures, or linguistic data created for the purpose of language documentation, preservation, educational language development, or licensed use in language-based applications including translation tools, AI systems that process natural language, and other language technology. Language itself is not owned. A language resource is owned only in its structure, selection, and arrangement.

3.11. **Educational Processing Candidate** — Any work submitted with a declared or requested intent for use in educational language systems, educational archives, or institutional processing and analysis systems. Educational Processing Candidate is a use declaration, not a standalone work type. It must be combined with one of the work types above. Eligibility for educational system use is controlled by classification, not by this designation alone. Where this designation is referred to as "AI Training Candidate" in other documents or systems, the same definition applies.

3.12. **Media Mapping Reference Table**

| Common Term | Legal Work Type |
|---|---|
| Book / Ebook | Literary Work |
| Song | Musical Work + Sound Recording |
| Film | Audiovisual Work |
| Radio Programme | Sound Recording + Broadcast |
| Television Programme | Audiovisual Work + Broadcast |
| Podcast | Sound Recording |
| Educational Textbook | Literary Work (Educational Material) |
| Dictionary / Encyclopedia | Reference Work |
| Dataset | Dataset or Compilation |
| Language Corpus | Language Resource |
| Educational System Processing Materials | Not a standalone type — governed by reproduction and database rules |

## 4. Definition of Folklore

4.1. Folklore is any expression of a community's cultural heritage that meets all of the following conditions:

- it originates from a collective or communal source;
- it has no identifiable individual author or composer; and
- it has been transmitted through generations as part of a community's cultural identity.

4.2. Expressions of folklore include, but are not limited to:

- traditional stories, legends, myths, and oral histories;
- traditional songs, melodies, rhythms, and musical forms;
- traditional dances, ceremonies, and ritual performances;
- traditional crafts, visual art forms, and design patterns;
- traditional proverbs, riddles, and oral expressions; and
- traditional knowledge systems directly tied to community identity.

4.3. Expressions of folklore are protected under the laws of The Gambia and are held in trust for the people of The Gambia. Folklore is not freely exploitable as private property.

4.4. Folklore is not a work type in the sense of Section 3. Folklore is a **classification category** that may apply across multiple work types. A sound recording of a traditional song may be assigned both the "Sound Recording" work type and the "Folklore" classification category.

4.5. Folklore is **not** defined or controlled by the aesthetic or stylistic qualities of a work. The presence of traditional style, rhythm, language, or cultural reference in a modern work does not make that work folklore.

## 5. Cultural Work vs. Modern Creation

### 5.1. The Locked Classification Logic

The following logic applies throughout the entire AIWA system and must not be altered, merged, simplified, or reinterpreted:

- **Modern work ≠ folklore.**
- **Cultural influence ≠ folklore.**
- **Folklore = collective origin + no identifiable author.**
- **Modern retelling = modern work.**

This logic is binding within AIWA governance and may only be altered by formal amendment to this document following the amendment procedure defined in AIWA-GOV-02. No officer, reviewer, or operational decision may deviate from this logic outside of that process.

### 5.2. Baseline Classification — Default Reality

5.2.1. Any submission is treated as a modern work if:

- there is an identifiable contributor;
- the work is fixed (recorded, written, captured); and
- it is submitted voluntarily.

5.2.2. The use of Mandinka, Wolof, Fula, or any other Gambian or West African language in a work does not change this classification.

5.2.3. The use of traditional rhythm, proverb, storytelling style, or cultural reference in a work does not change this classification.

5.2.4. Cultural influence is neutral. It is not a disqualifier and is not an automatic trigger for restricted handling. This rule protects creators from being erased or excluded based on cultural expression.

5.2.5. A dictionary, dataset, or language resource based on a Gambian language is a modern work. The language documented is not owned. The structure, selection, and arrangement of the resource are owned by the creator.

### 5.3. Elevated Classification — Risk Zone

5.3.1. A work is subject to elevated classification review when one or more of the following conditions are present:

- the work references known traditional expressions;
- the work uses proverbs, oral histories, or traditional narratives;
- the work resembles known traditional songs, stories, or ceremonies;
- the work shows communal authorship signals;
- ownership clarity is absent or contested.

5.3.2. **Dual Layer Recognition.** A work may be both:

- a modern copyrighted work; **and**
- a work that contains protected folklore elements.

These two conditions are not mutually exclusive. Both may apply simultaneously. When both apply, the work is classified as a **Folklore-Linked Modern Work**.

5.3.3. **No Automatic Downgrade.** A work that triggers elevated classification review does not automatically become folklore. The elevated flag does not remove the work's modern work status. It creates a separate review obligation.

5.3.4. **Controlled Ambiguity.** The law does not clearly define all boundary cases. Rhythms, motifs, phrases, and patterns may fall in unresolved zones. AIWA does not claim to resolve these zones by default. Where a work falls in an unresolved zone:

- the work is marked: **Requires Cultural Review Context**; and
- the mark is not a rejection, and it is not a reclassification to folklore.

### 5.4. Extreme Case Classification — State and Cultural Sensitivity

5.4.1. Extreme case classification applies when one or more of the following conditions are present:

- the material is sacred or ceremonially restricted;
- tribal or ethnic ownership claims exist or are likely;
- the content is tied to historical recordings connected to community identity;
- the content is government-recognized heritage;
- cross-border cultural claims exist or are foreseeable.

5.4.2. **State and Cultural Override Exists.** Some materials are not governed purely by copyright. They may be controlled by:

- the Ministry of Tourism and Culture;
- the National Centre for Arts and Culture (NCAC);
- community authority with recognized standing;
- state authority under applicable Gambian law.

5.4.3. **No Assumption of Permission.** When extreme classification is triggered, the default position is restricted status. The work remains restricted until it is formally cleared through the process defined in AIWA-DOC-03.

5.4.4. **Folklore is Controlled, Not Free.** Folklore is not "free use." It is controlled, licensable, and permission-based. A work designated as folklore remains subject to restriction, authorized use conditions, and community allocation requirements.

5.4.5. **Misclassification Risk.** Misclassification of extreme-case material may result in legal exposure, cultural conflict, and political escalation. This is not theoretical. This document is designed to prevent it.

## 6. Classification Categories

6.1. All works in the AIWA system must be assigned one of the following five classification categories. The classification category controls treatment, monetization eligibility, AI eligibility, and revenue routing.

### 6.1. Class 1 — Original Individual Work

6.1.1. A work created by an identifiable individual or group with no disputed folklore elements and no community-linked restriction.

6.1.2. Default treatment:

- May be monetized.
- May enter AI systems where otherwise permitted under rights clearance.
- Community allocation is not automatically required.

### 6.2. Class 2 — Culturally Derived Work

6.2.1. A modern work that draws on cultural references, styles, or traditions but is created by an identifiable contributor. The work does not reproduce protected traditional expressions directly.

6.2.2. Default treatment:

- May be monetized with review conditions.
- May enter AI systems with tagging and use limits.
- Attribution and revenue controls apply.

### 6.3. Class 3 — Community-Linked Work

6.3.1. A work that contains identifiable elements linked to a specific community's cultural heritage or that carries communal significance. The work may have an identifiable contributor, but its cultural link creates obligations.

6.3.2. Default treatment:

- Restricted unless formally cleared.
- May enter AI systems only with special approval.
- Community allocation layer applies.
- Any credible community objection triggers a pause and review.

### 6.4. Class 4 — Restricted Work

6.4.1. A work that is folklore, contains folklore, is sacred, is ceremonially restricted, or is otherwise blocked from standard use under Gambian law or AIWA policy.

6.4.2. Default treatment:

- Blocked from monetization unless specifically approved.
- Does not enter AI systems unless lawfully and specifically authorized.
- Community allocation layer is mandatory.

### 6.5. Class 4A — Government-Authorized Restricted Work

6.5.1. A restricted work for which a verified government authorization has been granted for specific, defined uses.

6.5.2. Government authorization:

- is operational permission, not ownership transfer;
- expands permitted use only to the exact uses stated;
- does not remove the underlying protection;
- does not apply to other works;
- expires unless formally renewed; and
- must be verified by AIWA before any use proceeds.

6.5.3. Default treatment:

- Permitted only for the exact uses stated in the verified authorization.
- Community allocation layer is mandatory.
- AI use eligibility is determined by the authorization, not by default.

## 7. Boundaries and Restrictions

### 7.1. AI Use Boundaries

7.1.1. AI use is not automatically permitted for any class of work.

7.1.2. AI use eligibility by class:

| Class | AI Use |
|---|---|
| Class 1 — Original Individual Work | Permitted where rights are clear |
| Class 2 — Culturally Derived Work | Permitted with tagging and defined limits |
| Class 3 — Community-Linked Work | Only with special approval |
| Class 4 — Restricted Work | Blocked unless lawfully and specifically authorized |
| Class 4A — Government-Authorized Restricted | Only if the verified authorization explicitly permits it |

7.1.3. Classification controls AI eligibility. A work may not enter an AI system ahead of its classification status.

7.1.4. Restricted and folklore-linked materials are blocked from AI use unless explicitly authorized.

### 7.2. Monetization Boundaries

7.2.1. Monetization eligibility by class:

| Class | Monetization |
|---|---|
| Class 1 — Original Individual Work | Permitted |
| Class 2 — Culturally Derived Work | Permitted with review conditions |
| Class 3 — Community-Linked Work | Restricted unless cleared |
| Class 4 — Restricted Work | Blocked unless specifically approved |
| Class 4A — Government-Authorized Restricted | Only within the scope of verified authorization |

### 7.3. Community Objection Rule

7.3.1. For any work identified as folklore, community-linked, or culturally sensitive:

- Any credible community objection pauses monetization immediately.
- The pause triggers a formal review under AIWA-DOC-03.
- The work remains paused until the review is completed.
- An objection is credible if it is accompanied by stated reasons, evidence, or recognized community standing.

7.3.2. A pause is not a permanent restriction. It is a hold pending review.

### 7.4. Revenue Handling for Restricted and Authorized Works

7.4.1. Restricted works (Class 4 and Class 4A) always require a community allocation layer where the work has a community-linked origin.

7.4.2. Community allocation applies:

- mandatorily for Class 4 and Class 4A works with communal origin; and
- for Class 3 works unless the contributor has elected not to contribute and no law or governance decision requires it.

### 7.5. Enforcement and Misdeclaration

7.5.1. False declarations by a contributor cause:

- immediate hold on the work;
- suspension of the contributor's access pending review; and
- revenue freeze for the affected work.

7.5.2. A first offense may be corrected if the evidence shows clear negligence rather than deliberate deception.

7.5.3. Deliberate misdeclaration is subject to removal and escalation.

## 8. Ownership Boundaries

8.0. **Language Non-Ownership Foundation.** AIWA does not claim ownership over any natural language, individual words, grammatical structures, public linguistic facts, or communal linguistic heritage. Rights asserted by AIWA apply only to original works, structured compilations, and system-level organizational arrangements created within the AIWA framework.

8.1. A work submitted to AIWA remains the property of its creator or rights holder as declared at intake.

8.2. Classification does not transfer, modify, or restrict the underlying ownership of a work.

8.3. AIWA's classification of a work does not grant AIWA ownership of that work.

8.4. Folklore is held in trust for the people of The Gambia. No individual, entity, or institution may claim private ownership of a work that is properly classified as folklore.

8.5. Government authorization does not transfer ownership of a folklore or restricted work to the government, to AIWA, or to any third party. It grants limited operational permission only.

8.6. A work's classification as Class 2, 3, 4, or 4A does not extinguish the creator's rights in the modern elements of that work. It imposes conditions on the exercise of those rights.

8.7. AIWA's involvement in offshore partnership structures does not affect AIWA's classification decisions. AIWA must classify works independently of any commercial interest.

8.8. Nothing in this document or in AIWA's classification, processing, or publication activities grants AIWA ownership over communal cultural heritage, sacred traditions, or community-owned cultural expressions. These remain held in trust for the people of The Gambia and the relevant communities.

## 9. Lexical Extraction — System Condition

9.1. **Universal Application.** All works submitted to AIWA are subject to lexical extraction as a condition of using AIWA services. This applies across all work types and all classification categories, including restricted and folklore-linked works.

9.2. **Nature of Lexical Extraction.** Lexical extraction is a technical and analytical process by which AIWA systems, operating through SPARXSTAR platform infrastructure (see AIWA-STD-01 for SPARXSTAR definition; see AIWA-DOC-02 Section 3A for AIWA-SPARXSTAR terminology alignment):

(a) identify and segment linguistic units including words, morphemes, tokens, and phrases;

(b) analyze relationships between such units including meaning, usage, and context; and

(c) convert such analysis into structured, statistical, or relational representations.

Lexical extraction does not involve preservation or reproduction of a submitted work in its original expressive form.

9.3. **Transformative Output.** Outputs of lexical extraction are transformed representations of language data. They are abstracted from the original expressive structure of the submitted work and are used to support indexing, linguistic analysis, educational archive construction, and educational language system operations. AIWA does not use lexical extraction to reconstruct or replicate submitted works in a form substantially similar to the original, except where necessary to provide a contributor-requested service such as returning the contributor's own content.

9.4. **AIWA Rights in Extracted Data.** AIWA holds rights in the structured organization, arrangement, and system-level representation of lexical extraction outputs as compiled within AIWA's linguistic datasets. AIWA does not claim ownership of:

(a) individual words, morphemes, or phrases;

(b) public linguistic facts or meanings; or

(c) language structures inherent to a natural language.

Rights attach to the structured dataset as a compiled and organized work, not to its individual linguistic components.

9.5. **Separation of Original Work and Extracted Data.**

| Layer | Rights Holder | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Original submitted work | Contributor or rights holder as declared | Governed by classification, publishing, and restriction rules |
| Lexical extraction output | AIWA (as structured system-level dataset) | Not the original work; not a substitute for the original work |

9.6. **TK and Sacred Content.** Lexical extraction of TK-classified (Traditional Knowledge — Class 4 or Class 4A restricted) or Sacred Content does not remove or bypass the applicable classification restrictions. Extracted outputs derived from restricted content remain subject to the same classification controls as the original work. AIWA will not surface extracted outputs from TK Red (Traditional Knowledge Red — the highest-restriction marker, equivalent to AIWA Class 4/4A: folklore, sacred, or ceremonially restricted content) or Sacred Content in general datasets, public resources, or AI systems from which the applicable restrictions have been removed.

9.7. **Submission as Acceptance.** Submission to AIWA constitutes the contributor's acceptance that lexical extraction will occur as a condition of the service. If a contributor does not accept lexical extraction, the contributor must not submit the work to AIWA.

9.8. **Non-Reconstruction Commitment.** AIWA does not intentionally design its systems to enable reconstruction of original submitted works from extracted or structured linguistic representations.

## 10. Confirmational Statements

10.1. Every contributor must affirm the following statements at the time of submission. These statements form part of the intake record.

10.2. **Statement A — Ownership**
"I confirm this work is created by me or I have the right to submit it."

10.3. **Statement B — Cultural Use**
"I confirm this work is not a direct reproduction of protected traditional expression without permission."

10.4. **Statement C — Folklore Awareness**
"I understand that if this work includes traditional cultural elements, it may require additional review or authorization."

10.5. **Statement D — Authority Acceptance**
"I acknowledge that certain cultural materials may be subject to community or government control."

10.6. **Statement E — Risk Transfer**
"I accept responsibility for the accuracy of this classification declaration."

10.7. Failure to affirm all five statements prevents the work from advancing past intake.

10.8. Affirmation of these statements does not eliminate AIWA's review obligation. It establishes the contributor's primary responsibility for accuracy.
